# NeedFinding

### 1. Original Problem Statement

Students who are just beginning to learn about biological knowledge in junior high school, or enthusiasts who have conducted research on TRPG groups often struggle with abstract proportional relationships, such as the surface-area-to-volume ratio in thermal regulation.

### 2. Context Description

2.1 Who the Users Are

The primary users are junior high school students (aged 12-15) who are beginning to explore biological evolution and ecosystem dynamics. Additionally, this includes TRPG (Tabletop Role-Playing Game) enthusiasts who are interested in simulation-heavy games but lack the specialized biological background to model complex scientific systems. These users possess basic logical reasoning but struggle to grasp non-linear relationships like the "surface-area-to-volume ratio" in thermal regulation.

2.2 When and Where

The problem manifests during standard biology classroom sessions, independent study of evolutionary units, or when players attempt to run science-themed TRPG sessions that involve ecological modeling or species survival mechanics.

2.3 What activity is being performed

Static Learning: Students typically analyze textbook case studies or perform pen-and-paper exercises. These activities are often deterministic and lack the chaos of real evolution.
Manual TRPG/Simulations: Enthusiasts may play games where they manually track stats and roll dice to simulate environmental changes. In these scenarios, the Game Master (GM) must act as the logic engine.

2.4 Concrete Conflict

In current TRPG or classroom simulations, when a species goes extinct due to a "Climate Dice" roll, the feedback is often purely numerical. There is a lack of scientific logic Interpretation to explain why a specific trait such as a high reproduction rate which was an asset during a growth phase, suddenly became a lethal liability during resource scarcity.

### 3. Need Finding Methods

To better understand the problem, a 15–20 minute semi-structured interview was conducted with a target user: a student with prior biology learning and experience with simulation games. The interview aimed to capture:

Their understanding of evolution and ecosystems
Current strategies for learning evolutionary concepts
Misconceptions and learning challenges
Feedback preferences and interactive learning experiences

During the interview, the student shared personal experiences, examples from educational games, and reflections on both abstract and concrete evolutionary processes.

### 4. Key Findings

Evolution is often misunderstood as goal-oriented
The student initially interpreted “survival of the fittest” as a moral or quality judgment (“good vs. bad genes”).
Through reflection, they recognized that survival depends on environmental suitability, not inherent gene “goodness.”

Insight: Learners need to see evolution as probabilistic adaptation rather than purposeful improvement.
Abstract and large-scale evolutionary changes are hard to visualize
Major changes (e.g., fish → amphibians, dinosaurs → birds) are difficult to comprehend due to long timescales and branching evolution.
Gradual changes or small-scale trait adaptations are easier to understand.

Insight: Learners struggle with scale and cumulative effects in evolution.
Interactive and visual materials aid comprehension
3D animations and videos showing step-by-step changes (e.g., limb modification in dinosaurs) help learners grasp abstract processes.
Textbooks using hierarchical classification assist in connecting traits and evolutionary lineage.

Insight: Visual and interactive representations are more effective than static text for understanding complex evolutionary concepts.
Learners want immediate feedback and causal explanations
When making hypothetical evolutionary decisions (e.g., adapting traits), the student preferred clear explanations: why a trait changed, what environmental factor caused it, and the consequences of the choice.

Insight: Feedback that links learner action to evolutionary outcome enhances understanding and engagement.
Simulation-like experiences increase engagement and understanding
Students valued the idea of controlling a species’ evolution and seeing consequences over simulated long timescales.
Experiencing sudden environmental changes (volcanoes, temperature shifts, sea-level rise) creates urgency and teaches survival consequences.

Insight: Interactive simulations that allow experimentation with evolution and environmental pressures support active learning.
Trade-offs and unintended consequences are important for learning
High reproduction rate or trait adaptation can have hidden downsides (e.g., spreading maladaptive genes, resource depletion).
Learners benefit from seeing both immediate and long-term consequences of decisions in ecosystems.

### 5. Refined Understanding of the Problem

The original problem statement focused on students’ misunderstanding of mutation and selection. The needfinding activity suggests a more nuanced problem:

Students struggle to visualize long-term and large-scale evolutionary processes.

They need interactive, immediate feedback to connect environmental changes with trait survival.

Misconceptions arise from abstract concepts, including probabilistic adaptation, trait trade-offs, and branching evolution.

Static classroom materials and passive observation (textbooks, lectures) are insufficient for grasping cumulative evolutionary dynamics.

Revised Problem Statement

Junior high students and biology enthusiasts struggle to understand evolution because abstract concepts, long timescales, and probabilistic adaptation make it difficult to visualize how traits survive or disappear. Learners need interactive, feedback-rich simulations that allow them to experiment with environmental changes, observe causal relationships, and understand evolutionary trade-offs over time.
